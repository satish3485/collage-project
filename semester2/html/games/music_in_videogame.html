Music in videogames

Introduction

A synthesiser from 70's

It is the soundtrack or background music that accompanies a video game.

In 1975, the first soundtracks were made with the use of the synthesiser technology; along with time, video games started to include also more complex kind of music, even full orchestral pieces.

The music in video games changes, according to the different situations in which the player is involved: the fight against enemies or the different places in which the protagonist of the game is.

1970: The first decade

In the late 70s, along with the golden age of arcade video games, music was stored on physical medium in analog waveforms (compact cassettes and phonograph records). Altough, the use of music was not so common because this means were very expensive and likely to be broken.

Gun Fight

The use of digital means was a breakthrough: electrical impulses changed the computer-code into analog-sound-waves to be used as output on a speaker. One of the first use of this technique was the opening chiptune in Tomohiro Nishikado's Gun Fight 2 (1975).

This allowed only monophonic, looped and short sounds as, for example, Pole Position or Pac-Man3 by Namco. On the contrary, in Space Invaders 4 by Taito a continuous background was used (there were four chromatic descending bass notes repeated in loop), the first melodic soundtrack is instead found in Rally X by Namco. The development in the field of music was so slow. Not every game programmer, in fact, had skills in music so the sound capabilities (also for home consoles) were limited. The famous Atari 2600, for example, only supported two tones at a time.

1980: The decisive breakthrough

During the 80s the game developers were getting used to this kind of technology. They began to improve their skills in this field trying different approaches: in Dig Dug 5 by Namco, for example, the music stops only when the protagonist is not moving, and Konami used eleven different tracks for their Frogger.

Home systems also saw a great upgrade in sound ability. The Commodore 64 in 1982 was capable of early forms of filtering effects. The Nintendo with their Nintendo Entertainment System (NES) in 1985, was capable of reproducing five different sound channels. Nintendo emerged also thanks to its collaboration with Koji Kondo6 that created the soundtracks for both Super Mario Bros.7 and The Legend Of Zelda 8 and with Hirokazu Tanaka9 for Metroid 10 and Kid Icarus.

iMuse technology

At the end of 1980 video game music were so popular that became to be sold as cassette tape soundtracks in Japan. Other companies from America started to give more attention to sounds in games.

Video game music became so popular in the 90s that LucasArts decided to build an engine dedicated to the musical aspects of games with his iMUSE. It was an interactive music system able to synchronise music with the visual action game, with transitions from one music theme to another.
