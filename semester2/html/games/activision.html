Activision

With the birth of the gaming industry, some companies gained popularity over others. Meanwhile some others began to stand out for their characteristic. This is the case of Activision, one of the most popular video game publisher companies of the 21st century.

Birth of Activision

Activision's building

During the 80s, third-party developers (companies that develop only the game and not the system) did not exist: the creators of a game's system were also its developers. One of the most famous example was the Atari company, who only developed games for the Atari 2600.

Many game designers were annoyed by this fact, as they did not receive any credit for their works, nor financial rewards for the number of games sold.

Atari programmers David Crane, Larry Kaplan, Alan Miller, and Bob Whitehead decided that this had gone on for long enough, and met Atari's CEO, Ray Kassar, to try and change this policy. They wanted to get royalties and their names on game boxes. Unfortunately Kassar did not approve their request.

This was the turning point: Crane, Miller, and Whitehead together left Atari and founded Activision in October 1979, including in their ambitius project the music industry executive Jim Levy and later also Kaplan.


Why was Actvision popular amongst programmers?

Alan Miller, David Crane and Garry Kitchen

First of all, creators of games were given the respect they deserved: in fact, the company promoted their own game designers and gave them full credit for their own creations.

An interesting addition was the possibility for players to send a photo with their high score in order to receive an embroidered patch in return.

Several important programmers were drawn to the Activision company due to its simple yet significant changes, allowing game designers to feel respected in their work environment.


What happened after the first years?

Pitfall
After having had some legal conflicts with Atari, Activision started developing their own titles. One of the most important of these was Pitfall! a video game for Atari 2006, whose seals were second only to Pac Man's.

Later on, Activision purchased the Infocom industry pioneer of text adventure, but that was not a good idea, because after several years of losses, in 1989 Activision shut down the Infocom studios in Cambridge, Massachusetts and most of the programmers lost their jobs in Activision.

Activision started getting involved in the development of other software during 1988 and as a result, changed its corporate name to Mediagenic, to have a name which would globally represent all its activities, even though they were still producing videogames for other companies.


Why did they choose the name Activision?

The name of the company does not have a particular meaning, as the founders mainly intended to ensure that the name of their new company appeared in the phone-book before Atari.
