Personal Details

Douglas Engelbart

Douglas Carl Engelbart1, born in Portland, Oregon, on January 30, 1925, was an american engineer and inventor, best known for inventing the computer mouse. He received his bachelor's degree in electrical engineering from Oregon State College and received a PhD from UC Berkley (University of California, Berkley).

Professional Accomplishments

He is best known for tackling the challenges of human-computer interaction. He founded the Augmentation Research center (ARC) Lab in SRI (Stanford research Institute) International. He also discovered a law based on the observation that the intrinsic rate of human performance is exponential, known as Engelbart's Law. His inventions were demonstrated at The Mother of All Demos2 in 1968.

He was greatly influenced by Vannevar Bush. It was only after reading Bush's article As we may think that Engelbart decided to explore the field of electrical engineering. By 1962, he had joined SRI International and proposed a report about his vision and proposed a research agenda entitled "Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework"

Engelbart embedded a set of organizing principles in his lab and termed them as the "bootstrapping strategy" - they were designed to boost the rate of innovation of his lab. ARC became the major force behind the design and development of the oN-Line System (NLS), where he and his team developed computer interface elements such as bitmapped screens, the computer mouse, hypertext, collaborative tools and precursors to the GUI. He applied for a patent for the wooden shell with two metal wheels (computer mouse), in 1967 and received it in 1970. He had developed it a few years earlier with his lead engineer, Bill English. Engelbart nicknamed it the "mouse" because the tail came out at the end. He never recieved any royalties for his invention.

In 1988, after Tymshare, Inc. gained control of ARC, Engelbart teamed up with his daughter Christina and founded the Bootstrap Institute to coalesce his ideas into a series of three day management seminars offered at Stanford between 1989 and 2000. Later, its name was changed to Doug Engelbart Institute.

Engelbart received several awards during his lifetime; the most noteworthy of which were the Turing award(1997), Lovelace medal(2005), National medal of technology(2000), and Lamelson-MIT Prize. He died on July 2, 2013 at the age of 88.
