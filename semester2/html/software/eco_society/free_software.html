Free software

The term free software refers to software that gives the user the freedom to run the software for any purpose as well as to study, modify, and distribute the original software and any adapted versions. Though the name would be officially coined only in 1983, forms of distribution and communities comparable to the free software community already existed long before the birth of the movement. For all its early history, software had mostly been distributed and shared freely by researchers (the only people to use computers at the time). This was not necessarily for ethical reasons, but mostly because it was used and made by scientists to do research, rather than sold by companies to make money; and because software was often distributed with or as source code, often coming with the machine itself.

Early decline

By the early 70s, free software was in decline. Due to the increasing complexity of operating systems and language compilers, the production cost of software had steadily increased over the previous decades. This brought to the emergence of software companies and the software industry, now increasingly disjoint from the hardware industry, and the decline of software bundled with hardware.

Though some software continued to be freely distributed and developed during the decade (in 1978 Donald Knuth released TeX, for example), licenses grew in numbers and restrictiveness, and copyright was enforced with increasing zeal. It was in 1976 that Bill Gates published his Open Letter to Hobbyists, complaining about the unauthorised sharing of a pre-release copy of Microsoft's BASIC interpreter.

The advent of the internet

During the 70s and early 80s free software had continued to be distributed as source code, mostly via magazines, books and tapes (notably the DECUS tapes). People even copied source code by hand from books and newspapers. However, with the advent of Usenet and UUCPNet, a new and convenient way of distributing software was born: the internet. This not only made sharing easier but also allowed for increased cooperation within the community.

The free software movement

Richard Stallman
GNU

In 1983, Richard Stallman, a computer programmer, launched the GNU Project, formally founding the free-software movement and coining the term free software. The GNU Project1 (GNU stands for GNU's Not Unix) aim was to create a complete Unix-like operating system, whose source code would be freely available and modifiable by the users. Stallman had come to this decision after several incidents, including one in which he and his colleagues were unable to fix their printer, because its driver's source code was publicly unavailable, restricted by licenses.
Two years later Stallman published the GNU Manifesto, outlining the aims of the GNU project, its importance and benefits to computer users, as well as answer to specific objection to GNU's goals and philosophy, and explaining the free software philosophy.
In 1985 the Free Software Foundation2 was founded, with the aim of promoting the freedom to study, distribute, create and modify software.
A year later The Free Software Definition was written by Stallman, explaining that the term free in free software is intended as in freedom rather than free of charge, and that once should be able to change a program, in order to control it, rather than being controlled by it: affirming the ethical rather than purely economical nature of the idea.
In the following years a license, the GNU General Public License, was developed and released in 1989, as a way to guarantee the rights contained in the Free Software Definition to the program recipient.
Finally, in 1991, Linus Torvalds released the source code for the Linux kernel, providing the final piece for the GNU Project.
