The evolution of users

There is no reason for any individual to have a computer in his home
- Ken Olsen, co-founder of the Digital Equipment Corporation, 1977

In the years between the 70s and the 90s many things changed in the world of computers. One of these great changes was the users themselves: due to the development of different ways of interacting with software and computers, lower prices and the growing usefulness of computers, the user demographic evolved in a drastic way.

Why would you ever want one?

The future, or so they thought

In the 70s it was the firm belief of many people that computers had no place in anyone's home, they were serious tools for serious matters and serious people, like scientists, researchers, and corporations. With the release of the first microcomputers, the Apple II and Commodore PET, this attitude remained, but, slowly, some began to dream of a different future, a future with a computer in every home, running software that would take care of every detail, and robots to take out the thrash. Computers were becoming cheaper, more powerful, and somewhat fashionable.
The idea of software that would allow mothers to store recipes and connect to medical databases to help with childcare, that would allow children to play video games, fathers to read personalised newspapers and homes to become intelligent, was spreading, and, consequentially, computers started spreading to different audiences, no longer just the scientists and enthusiasts of the early days.

I told you they were useless

Unfortunately for the futuristic dreams of '77, reality ensued: the home computers of the late 70s and early 80s were not powerful enough to handle dreamy automated houses and they were not particularly usable, with at best text-based interfaces and at worst command-line interfaces that required the memorisation of multiple commands. So, after the video game crash of 1983, computers were no longer the fashionable machine of the futuristic man, but at most the toy of quite unfashionable nerds, at best the tools of scientists and businesses. The home computers purchased in the early optimistic atmosphere were abandoned or left as toys for children.
The user demographic was thus practically returned to its original form: scientists, corporations and enthusiasts.

Not so useless after all

Despite the backlash of the early 80s, computers survived, hardware kept becoming cheaper and more powerful, and an ever increasing number of businesses and corporations started adopting them. On the software side, developments in user interface, like the GUI, had become ubiquitous by the late 80s, making computers much more approachable and usable by everyone. The advent of the Internet also made computers much more interesting for the general public. So, in the early 90s people started buying computers once again, enticed by the ability to work at home, using the same operating system, usually MS-DOS, and programs they used in the office, play games and surf the internet.

A steep price

Back in the early 70s computers were expensive, machines used by scientists, a few businesses and some enthusiast, but by the early 90s things had completely changed, computers were more widespread and usable and so the user demographic changed too: now a sizeable chunk of the user base had no idea about how computers worked, how programs were written or what a command-line even was.
While cheaper hardware was certainly a main driver of this demographic shift, GUIs were absolutely key in allowing the average desk worker to become the average user. This made computers successful, but it also had a steep price: if in the 70s BASIC was the lingua franca of computers and practically every user knew it, by the early 90s, more and more users didn't even know what BASIC was, which would have forced software developers to make programs even more usable, and hide away the true nature of computers and software.
