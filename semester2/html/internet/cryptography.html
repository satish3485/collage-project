Cryptography

Data Encryption Standard (DES)

The DES was once a predominant symmetric-key algorithm for encryption of electronic data. DES was developed in the early 1970s at IBM and also was based on an earlier design by Horst Feistel and then this algorithm was submitted to National Bureau of Standards (NBS) following the agency’s invitation to propose a candidate for the protection of sensitive government data. On 17 March 1975, the proposed DES was published in the Federal Register.Also DES is the archetypal block cipher- and algorithm that takes a fixed-length string of plaintext bits and transforms it through a series of complicated operations into another cipher text bit string of the same length. In 1976, according to the consultation with NSA, the NBS eventually selected a modified version, which was published as an official Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS) for the United States in 1977. DES is now considered to be insecure for many applications.

Cryptanalysis

Public-key cryptography

For breaking full 16 rounds of DES with less complexity than a brute-force search, there are known three attacks: differential cryptanalysis (DC), linear cryptanalysis (LC), and Davies’ attack. DC was rediscovered in 1980s by Eli Biham and Adi Shamir. And also DC was known earlier to IBM and the NSA and kept secret. LC was discovered by Mitsuru Matsui and this method was implemented and also was the first experimental cryptanalysis of DES to be reported. Davies’ attack is different from previous ones because they were general techniques and can be applied to a number of schemes, but Davies’ attack was a specialised technique for DES. Donald Davies was first who suggested it in the eighties and it also was improved by Biham and Biryukov.

Public-key cryptography

Public-key cryptography, also known as asymmetric cryptography, is a class of cryptographic algorithms which required two separate keys. First one is a secret (or private) and the second one is public. The public key is used to verify a digital signature; whereas the private key is used to decrypt cipher text or to create a digital signature. Public-key algorithms are fundamental security parts in crypto systems, protocols and applications. They underpin various Internet standards, such as PGP and GPG.
