Telnet

History

Network diagram for Telnet connection

Telnet is a network protocol used on the Internet or local area networks to provide a bidirectional interactive text-oriented communication facility using virtual terminal connection. Telnet was developed in 1968. Before 1973, Telnet was an ad hoc protocol with no official definition. It used an 8-bit channels to exchange 7-bit ASCII data.

Telnet Protocol Specification

On March 5, 1973, Telnet protocol standard was defined at UCLA with the public of two NIC documents: Telnet Protocol Specification and Telnet Option Specifications. Historically, Telnet provided access to a command-line interface on a remote host. Most operating systems with a TCP/IP stack support a Telnet service for remote configuration. The term telnet sometimes refer to the software which implements the client part of the protocol. Also, to telnet means to establish a connection with the Telnet protocol, either with command line client or with a programmatic interface. There were also attached specifications for Telnet options which allow negotiation of binary transmission, echoing, reconnection, suppression of “Go Ahead”, approximate message size, use of a “timing mark”, discussion of status and extension of option code set. Dave Walden prepared these specifications with the help of Bernie Cosell, Ray Tomlinson and Bob Thomas. Also these specifications have been prepared by Jerry Burchfiel and by David Crocker. These options cover most of the capabilities of the current version of Telnet.

Security

When Telnet was developed, most users of networked computers were in the computer departments of academic institutions, or a large private and government research facilities. After bandwidth explosion of the 1990s, the rise in number of people with access to the Internet, and by extension the number of people attempting to hack other people’s servers, made encrypted alternatives necessary. So computer security experts, such as SANS Institute, recommended that the use of Telnet for remote logins shout be discontinued under all normal circumstances.
