Keyboard Hardware

Origins

From the 1940s until the late 1960s, typewriters were the main means of data entry and output for computing, becoming integrated into what were known as computer terminals. Because of the limitations of terminals based upon printed text in comparison to the growth in data storage, processing and transmission, a general move towards video-based computer terminals was made near the 1970s.

Evolution

The Data point 3300

By the early 1970s, it was realized that an affordable video data entry terminal could replace the ubiquitous punched cards and allow for greater interaction with computers. Displaying the information at video speeds and the amount of memory needed to store the information on a page were the main problems, and the Data point 3300 from Computer Terminal Corporation introduced in 1967 was the first one to address these problems by using a digital shift-register design.

The competitors

In the mid-1970s, Imsai and Altair created the first small PCS for consumers. Their keyboard was located on the front panel of the computer, as a set of key switches. If users wanted a standard keyboard, they could look to IBM for its converted electric typewriter. However, there was a limited supply as there wasn't a high demand for them. Hence, most users had to convert their own electric typewriters if they wanted to have a standard keyboard. In the late 1970s Apple, Radio Shack and Commodore all foresaw the great role that computer keyboards could play in the market, and started manufacturing keyboards for their computers, paving the way for the modern assumption that all computers should come with a keyboard and that keyboards are the primary and standard input device.

Keyboard of Today

The IBM model M

In 1984, IBM introduced the Model M keyboard. This computer keyboard became popular amongst users as it was very easy to use. Also, users didn’t need to convert their typewriters or build their own keyboards to use as an input device for their computers anymore. The Model M was a mechanical keyboard, and it used the highest quality construction, giving typists the satisfaction of tactile feedback, great accuracy and comfort.

Membrane keyboards

In the 1990s, membrane switches began to replace the mechanical key switch, as it was quieter, weighed less, and suited the needs of the new laptop generation to come. This was also an advantage for manufactures as membrane keyboards were much cheaper to produce.
