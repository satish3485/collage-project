MITS Altair 8800

Why is it important?

The Altair 8800 is considered the very first personal computer in the history of computer science: it was affordable and easy to obtain. Before the Altair 8800 the word "computer" was related to big calculators, very expensive and not purchasable for personal use. It was featured in 1975 by the MITS (Micro Instrumentation & Telemetry Systems) in the magazine Popular Electronics and its inventor, Edward Roberts, created it with the hope that "one day I will give a computer to every person".

What was it like?

Inner components

The innovation of the Altair 8800 was that, with a supplement of 100$, you could buy the computer ready-assembled. You could also buy it unassembled and you had to assemble it by yourself and you could add some extras like an expanded memory, a teletype terminal or an Audio Cassette interface board. When the Altair made its first appearance, it was composed by a metal box and inside a power source, a front panel and a motherboard, made of slots, that was connected with the CPU (an Intel 8080 microprocessor) and the main memory, which was placed on separate slots. Inside the ROM was inserted an Altair BASIC programming language (a simplified version of the standard BASIC).

How it worked

The front panel was made of a big number of switches and LEDs: the only thing that you could do was make programs in binary using the switches and see the result with the LEDs.
